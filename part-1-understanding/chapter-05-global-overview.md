# Chapter 4: Global Overview - Earth's Volcanic Inventory

## Counting Fire Mountains: A Complex Question

How many volcanoes exist on Earth? Ask a volcanologist this seemingly simple question and you'll get different answers depending on what—and how—you're counting. The total could be 1,500, 100,000, or even a million, depending on your criteria.

### Why Counting Volcanoes Is Complicated

The challenge comes down to three key questions:

1. **What counts as a "volcano"?** Is it a single vent, a volcanic system, or an entire field?
2. **What does "active" mean?** Erupted yesterday? This century? Within human history?
3. **Where are you counting?** Just on land, or underwater too?

Let's break down the different ways to count Earth's volcanoes.

## The Official Count: Holocene Active Volcanoes

According to the Smithsonian Institution's Global Volcanism Program (as of 2025):

- **1,229 volcanoes** with Holocene eruptions (eruptions within the last 11,700 years)
- **856 volcanoes** with confirmed historical eruptions (documented by humans)
- **~1,350 potentially active volcanoes** worldwide (USGS estimate, excluding mid-ocean ridges)
- **45-58 volcanoes** erupting at any given time
- **~500 volcanoes** have erupted since 1800

**This is the standard count** used by scientists and in this book. These are discrete volcanic edifices (mountains, cones, or calderas) that have erupted since the end of the last Ice Age.

### Defining "Active"

Volcanologists consider a volcano "active" if it has erupted during the **Holocene epoch** (the past 11,700 years). This might seem arbitrary, but it represents the period since the last Ice Age ended—the timeframe of human civilization.

A volcano can be:
- **Active**: Has erupted in the Holocene and is likely to erupt again
- **Dormant**: Not currently erupting but could awaken (sometimes included in "active" counts)
- **Extinct**: Unlikely to ever erupt again (though this is hard to prove definitively)

The distinction between dormant and extinct can be fuzzy. Some volcanoes sleep for thousands of years between eruptions. Mount Pinatubo in the Philippines had no confirmed historical eruptions before its massive VEI 6 eruption in 1991—it had been dormant for 500 years.

## The Underwater Majority: Submarine Volcanoes

The ~1,350 count only includes **subaerial volcanoes** (above sea level). The ocean hides the majority of Earth's volcanic activity:

### Mid-Ocean Ridge Volcanoes
- **65,000 km** of volcanic ridges encircling the planet
- **Thousands of individual volcanic vents** along these ridges
- **~75% of Earth's annual magma output** occurs here
- Create all new oceanic crust (about 20 km³ per year)
- Most eruptions go completely unobserved by humans

### Seamounts (Submarine Volcanic Mountains)
- **~100,000 seamounts** taller than 1 km identified so far
- **Potentially 25 million** smaller submarine volcanoes exist
- Most are extinct or dormant
- A few dozen are known to be actively erupting

If we include submarine volcanoes, the count jumps to **well over 100,000 volcanic features**.

## Volcanic Systems vs. Individual Volcanoes

Counting gets more complex when dealing with volcanic systems:

### Iceland's Volcanic Systems
Iceland has **30-35 volcanic systems**, but these contain hundreds of individual vents, fissures, and craters. Is Iceland 30 volcanoes or 200+?

### Yellowstone
Is Yellowstone one supervolcano or a volcanic field with dozens of features? The caldera contains hundreds of hot springs, geysers, and hydrothermal features.

### Mount Etna
Etna is counted as one volcano, but it has four active summit craters plus dozens of lateral vents that have erupted independently.

**Standard practice**: Count discrete volcanic edifices (mountains) as single volcanoes, even if they have multiple vents.

## The Complete Planetary Inventory

If we count **every volcanic feature** that has ever existed on Earth:

### On Land
- **~1,350 active/potentially active volcanoes** (Holocene)
- **~10,000-50,000 extinct volcanoes** still visible (Pleistocene and older)
- **Hundreds of thousands** of deeply eroded volcanic features
- **Thousands** of volcanic fields, each containing multiple cones

### Underwater
- **~100,000 mapped seamounts** >1 km tall
- **~25 million estimated** smaller submarine volcanoes
- **Continuous volcanic ridges** spanning 65,000 km

### Total Estimate
**500,000 to over 1 million** discrete volcanic features have formed throughout Earth's history, though most are now extinct and many are completely eroded away.

## Why This Book Uses the "1,350" Number

This book focuses on the **~1,350 Holocene active volcanoes** because these are the ones that:

1. **Matter to modern humans**: They can erupt during our lifetimes
2. **Can be studied**: We can observe and monitor them
3. **Can be visited**: Adventure-seekers can experience them
4. **Are scientifically catalogued**: We have detailed data on them
5. **Shape current geography**: They're actively building/destroying land today

The other hundreds of thousands of volcanic features are geologically interesting but don't actively threaten populations or offer the same opportunities for study and adventure.

## Regional Distribution of Active Volcanoes

Understanding where these ~1,350 active volcanoes are located reveals Earth's tectonic patterns:

## The Ring of Fire: Earth's Volcanic Powerhouse

The Pacific "Ring of Fire" dominates global volcanism:

- **693 Holocene volcanoes** (56% of all active volcanoes)
- **41 different volcanic regions**
- **90% of Earth's earthquakes**
- **75% of the world's active and dormant volcanoes**

This horseshoe-shaped belt stretches 40,000 km around the Pacific Ocean, from New Zealand through the East Indies, Japan, the Aleutians, and down the western coasts of North, Central, and South America.

### Ring of Fire by the Numbers

Since 1800, the Ring of Fire has produced:
- **3,492 eruptions** (65% of global total)
- **1,534 eruptions since 1960** (68% of global total)

## Regional Distribution

### Asia-Pacific: The Volcanic Heartland

**Most Ring of Fire volcanoes are in Asia** (over 400, including submarine):

- **Indonesia**: 81 active volcanoes (the most of any country)
- **Japan**: 81 active volcanoes
- **Russia (Kamchatka & Kurils)**: 109 volcanoes
- **Philippines**: ~50 active volcanoes
- **Papua New Guinea**: ~40 active volcanoes
- **US Territory (Marianas, Guam)**: 25 volcanoes

**Why so many?** The western Pacific hosts numerous subduction zones where oceanic plates descend into the mantle, creating ideal conditions for volcanism.

### North America: The Eastern Ring

**~209 Ring of Fire volcanoes in North America**:

- **USA**: Over 100 volcanoes
  - Alaska: ~130 volcanoes (including Aleutian Islands)
  - Cascades: ~20 major volcanoes
  - Hawaii: 5 active volcanoes (hot spot, not Ring of Fire)
  - Yellowstone: Active supervolcano
- **Canada**: ~21 potentially active volcanoes (mostly in British Columbia)
- **Mexico**: ~40 active volcanoes
- **Central America**: ~80 active volcanoes (Guatemala, El Salvador, Nicaragua, Costa Rica)

### South America: The Andean Powerhouse

**~152 Ring of Fire volcanoes** along the Andes:

- **Chile**: 71 active volcanoes (including Chile-Argentina border)
- **Ecuador**: 21 active volcanoes
- **Peru**: ~16 active volcanoes
- **Colombia**: ~15 active volcanoes
- **Bolivia**: Several active volcanoes
- **Argentina**: ~18 (including shared with Chile)

The Andes host some of the world's highest volcanoes, with over 20 peaks exceeding 6,000 meters.

### Europe and Mediterranean: Classical Fire

- **Italy**: ~10 active volcanoes (Etna, Vesuvius, Stromboli, Vulcano)
- **Greece**: Santorini, Nisyros, Methana
- **Iceland**: ~30 volcanic systems (Mid-Atlantic Ridge)
- **Azores (Portugal)**: 9 volcanic islands
- **Canary Islands (Spain)**: 8 volcanic islands

### Africa: The Great Rift

- **East African Rift**: ~100 volcanoes
  - Ethiopia: ~60 volcanoes
  - Kenya: Kilimanjaro (5,895 m), Mount Kenya
  - Democratic Republic of Congo: Nyiragongo, Nyamuragira
  - Tanzania: Ol Doinyo Lengai (the only volcano that erupts natrocarbonatite lava)

### Antarctica and Remote Islands

- **Antarctica**: ~138 volcanoes (including many under ice)
  - Mount Erebus: The southernmost active volcano (continuously erupting)
- **South Sandwich Islands**: ~20 volcanoes
- **Indian Ocean**: Réunion, Comoros, Heard Island

## Eruption Frequency: How Often Do Volcanoes Erupt?

### Current Activity (2025)
- **40-60 volcanoes** erupting at any given time
- **10-20 volcanoes** producing continuous or frequent activity (Stromboli, Kilauea, Etna, Erebus, etc.)

### Historical Patterns
On average:
- **50-70 volcanoes** erupt each year
- **160 volcanoes** are active in any given decade
- **~550 volcanoes** have erupted in recorded history

### The Most Active Volcanoes

Some volcanoes erupt almost constantly:

1. **Stromboli (Italy)**: Erupting continuously for at least 2,000 years
2. **Mount Erebus (Antarctica)**: Continuous lava lake activity
3. **Kilauea (Hawaii)**: Erupted almost continuously from 1983-2018
4. **Etna (Italy)**: Frequent eruptions, active most years
5. **Nyiragongo (DR Congo)**: Contains the world's largest lava lake
6. **Yasur (Vanuatu)**: Called the "lighthouse of the Pacific" for its regular eruptions

## VEI: Measuring Volcanic Explosiveness

The **Volcanic Explosivity Index (VEI)** ranks eruptions from 0 (non-explosive) to 8 (mega-colossal):

| VEI | Description | Plume Height | Volume | Frequency | Example |
|-----|-------------|--------------|--------|-----------|---------|
| 0 | Effusive | <100 m | <10,000 m³ | Daily | Hawaiian lava flows |
| 1 | Gentle | 100-1,000 m | >10,000 m³ | Daily | Stromboli |
| 2 | Explosive | 1-5 km | >1,000,000 m³ | Weekly | Galeras (2010) |
| 3 | Severe | 3-15 km | >10,000,000 m³ | Yearly | Nevado del Ruiz (1985) |
| 4 | Cataclysmic | 10-25 km | >0.1 km³ | ≥10 years | Eyjafjallajökull (2010) |
| 5 | Paroxysmal | >25 km | >1 km³ | ≥50 years | Mount St. Helens (1980) |
| 6 | Colossal | >25 km | >10 km³ | ≥100 years | Pinatubo (1991), Krakatoa (1883) |
| 7 | Super-colossal | >25 km | >100 km³ | ≥1,000 years | Tambora (1815) |
| 8 | Mega-colossal | >25 km | >1,000 km³ | ≥10,000 years | Yellowstone (640,000 years ago) |

**Important note**: VEI is logarithmic. Each step up represents a 10x increase in erupted material.

### Detailed VEI Scale Rankings: Notable Historical Eruptions

#### VEI 8: Mega-Colossal (Supervolcano Eruptions)

**No recorded historical eruptions**. All VEI 8 eruptions occurred in prehistoric times:

- **Yellowstone, USA** (640,000 years ago): 1,000 km³
- **Yellowstone, USA** (2.1 million years ago): 2,450 km³
- **Toba, Indonesia** (74,000 years ago): 2,800 km³ - May have caused volcanic winter and human population bottleneck
- **Taupo, New Zealand** (~26,500 years ago): 1,170 km³
- **Lake Toba** formed the largest volcanic lake on Earth

**Threshold**: >1,000 km³ of ejected material

#### VEI 7: Super-Colossal

**Last occurrence**: Mount Tambora, 1815

**Historical eruptions** (past 10,000 years):
- **Mount Tambora, Indonesia** (1815): 160 km³ - Largest eruption in recorded history
  - Killed 71,000+ directly
  - Caused "Year Without a Summer" (1816)
  - Global temperature dropped 0.4-0.7°C
  - Crop failures and famine killed 100,000+ more

- **Mount Rinjani (Samalas), Indonesia** (1257): ~40 km³
  - May have triggered the Little Ice Age
  - Largely forgotten eruption rediscovered by scientists

- **Taupo, New Zealand** (232 CE): ~120 km³
  - Most violent eruption in past 5,000 years
  - Visible from China (recorded in Chinese texts)

- **Crater Lake (Mount Mazama), USA** (~5677 BCE): ~116 km³
  - Created Crater Lake caldera

**Pre-historic VEI 7 eruptions**:
- Campi Flegrei, Italy (~39,000 years ago)
- Long Valley Caldera, USA (~760,000 years ago)
- Valles Caldera, USA (~1.25 million years ago)

**Frequency**: Roughly every 1,000-10,000 years

#### VEI 6: Colossal

**Major historical eruptions**:

- **Pinatubo, Philippines** (1991): 10 km³
  - Second-largest eruption of 20th century
  - Cooled Earth by 0.5°C for 1-2 years
  - Successfully evacuated 60,000+ people
  - Destroyed US Clark Air Base

- **Krakatoa, Indonesia** (1883): 25 km³
  - Killed 36,000+ people (mostly from tsunami)
  - Explosion heard 4,800 km away (loudest sound in recorded history)
  - Tsunamis up to 40 m high
  - Red sunsets worldwide for years

- **Novarupta/Katmai, Alaska** (1912): 15 km³
  - Largest eruption of 20th century
  - No direct casualties (remote location)
  - Created Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes

- **Santa María, Guatemala** (1902): 8.5 km³
  - Killed 5,000-6,000 people
  - Santiaguito lava dome still growing

- **Huaynaputina, Peru** (1600): ~19 km³
  - Largest eruption in South American history
  - Caused global cooling
  - Crop failures in Russia led to famine

- **Hunga Tonga-Hunga Ha'apai, Tonga** (2022): ~10 km³
  - Largest eruption since Pinatubo
  - Massive underwater eruption
  - Tsunami affected Pacific region
  - Atmospheric shockwave circled Earth multiple times

**Other VEI 6 eruptions**:
- Long Island, Papua New Guinea (~1660): Possibly inspired "time of darkness" legends
- Quilotoa, Ecuador (~1280)
- Baitoushan (Changbaishan), China-North Korea border (~969)

**Frequency**: Roughly every 50-100 years

#### VEI 5: Paroxysmal

**Notable eruptions** (past 150 years):

- **Mount St. Helens, USA** (1980): 1.2 km³
  - Lateral blast removed 400 m from summit
  - Killed 57 people
  - Most documented eruption in history

- **El Chichón, Mexico** (1982): 0.8 km³
  - Killed ~2,000 people
  - Surprise eruption from "dormant" volcano

- **Cerro Hudson, Chile** (1991): 4.3 km³
  - Chile's second-largest 20th-century eruption

- **Galunggung, Indonesia** (1982): 0.5 km³
  - Two Boeing 747 incidents (ash ingestion)
  - Led to improved volcanic ash warning systems for aviation

- **Kelud, Indonesia** (1919): 0.15 km³
  - Killed ~5,000 people from lahars

- **Vesuvius, Italy** (79 CE): ~4 km³
  - Buried Pompeii and Herculaneum
  - Killed ~16,000 people
  - First scientific description (Pliny the Younger)
  - Defined "Plinian eruption"

**Frequency**: Roughly every 10-12 years

#### VEI 4: Cataclysmic

**Recent examples**:

- **Eyjafjallajökull, Iceland** (2010): 0.27 km³
  - Disrupted European air travel for 6 days
  - 100,000 flights canceled
  - Demonstrated modern vulnerability to moderate eruptions

- **Mayon, Philippines** (1814): ~0.5 km³
  - Buried town of Cagsawa
  - Killed ~1,200 people

- **Calbuco, Chile** (2015): 0.3 km³
  - Surprise eruption with minimal warning
  - Spectacular photos/videos

- **Kelud, Indonesia** (2014): 0.17 km³
  - Evacuated 200,000+ people

- **Merapi, Indonesia** (2010): 0.14 km³
  - Killed 350+ people
  - Pyroclastic flows

- **Chaitén, Chile** (2008): 1.0 km³
  - First eruption in 9,400 years
  - Rhyolitic eruption (rare composition)

**Frequency**: Multiple times per year globally

#### VEI 0-3: Daily to Yearly Occurrences

**VEI 3 examples**:
- Nevado del Ruiz, Colombia (1985) - Killed 23,000 from lahars despite VEI 3
- Ontake, Japan (2014) - Killed 63 hikers (deadliest in Japan since 1902)

**VEI 1-2**: Stromboli (continuous), Sakurajima (hundreds/year), Semeru (frequent)

**VEI 0**: Hawaiian lava flows, Erta Ale lava lake

**Frequency**: Thousands per year globally

## Submarine Volcanism: The Hidden Majority

While we focus on visible, subaerial volcanoes, most volcanism occurs underwater:

### Mid-Ocean Ridges
- **65,000 km** of volcanic ridges

## Supervolcanoes: The Ultimate Volcanic Threat

### What Makes a Supervolcano?

A "supervolcano" isn't a specific type of volcano—it's a volcanic system capable of producing eruptions **at least VEI 8**, ejecting more than **1,000 km³** of material in a single event. For context:

- **Mount St. Helens (1980)**: 1.2 km³ (VEI 5)
- **Pinatubo (1991)**: 10 km³ (VEI 6)
- **Tambora (1815)**: 160 km³ (VEI 7)
- **Yellowstone (640,000 ya)**: 1,000 km³ (VEI 8) - **800x larger than Pinatubo**

A VEI 8 eruption would be **catastrophic** on a global scale.

### Earth's Known Supervolcanoes

#### 1. Yellowstone Caldera (USA)

**Location**: Wyoming, Montana, Idaho
**Caldera size**: 55 × 72 km
**Last VEI 8**: 640,000 years ago

**Eruption history**:
- **2.1 million years ago**: 2,450 km³ (Huckleberry Ridge eruption) - Largest known Yellowstone eruption
- **1.3 million years ago**: 280 km³ (Mesa Falls eruption)
- **640,000 years ago**: 1,000 km³ (Lava Creek eruption) - Created current caldera

**Current status**:
- **Not expected to erupt soon** (no magma mobilization detected)
- ~1,000-3,000 earthquakes per year (normal background seismicity)
- Ground rises and falls over decades ("breathing")
- Massive hydrothermal system (10,000+ hot springs, geysers)
- Continuously monitored by USGS

**If it erupted**:
- Ash would cover most of USA (10+ cm over western states)
- Global cooling for years
- Crop failures worldwide
- Millions at risk
- Economic catastrophe

**But**: No signs of imminent eruption. Recurrence interval ~600,000-800,000 years.

#### 2. Lake Toba (Indonesia)

**Location**: North Sumatra
**Caldera size**: 100 × 30 km (largest volcanic lake on Earth)
**Last VEI 8**: ~74,000 years ago

**The 74,000 year eruption**:
- **2,800 km³** ejected (largest eruption in past 2.5 million years)
- Created massive caldera, now Lake Toba
- May have caused **volcanic winter** lasting 6-10 years
- **Genetic bottleneck theory**: Human population may have dropped to 3,000-10,000 individuals
- Evidence for severe climate impact debated by scientists

**Current status**:
- Dormant
- Island in lake (Samosir) is resurgent dome
- Geothermal activity
- No signs of imminent eruption

#### 3. Taupo Volcanic Zone (New Zealand)

**Location**: North Island, New Zealand
**Caldera size**: 35 × 30 km (Lake Taupo)

**Major eruptions**:
- **~26,500 years ago (Oruanui)**: 1,170 km³ (VEI 8)
- **232 CE (Hatepe)**: ~120 km³ (VEI 7) - Most violent eruption in past 5,000 years

**The 232 CE eruption**:
- Visible from China (recorded in texts)
- Sky turned red worldwide
- Pyroclastic flows covered 20,000 km²
- Created modern Lake Taupo configuration

**Current status**:
- Active volcanic zone
- Frequent smaller eruptions from nearby volcanoes
- Closely monitored

#### 4. Campi Flegrei (Italy)

**Location**: West of Naples (Bay of Naples)
**Caldera size**: ~12 km diameter
**Population at risk**: 3.5+ million

**Major eruptions**:
- **~39,000 years ago (Campanian Ignimbrite)**: ~300 km³ (VEI 7-8)
  - May have contributed to Neanderthal extinction in Europe
  - Ash deposited across Mediterranean and Eastern Europe
- **~15,000 years ago (Neapolitan Yellow Tuff)**: ~40 km³

**Current status**:
- **Showing signs of unrest**
- Bradyseism: Ground rises/falls by meters over years
- Increased seismic activity in recent decades
- Parts of Naples built inside caldera
- Most dangerous supervolcano due to population density

**If it erupted**:
- Millions at immediate risk
- Southern Europe catastrophically affected
- Mediterranean climate disruption

#### 5. Long Valley Caldera (California, USA)

**Location**: Eastern California (near Mammoth Lakes)
**Caldera size**: 32 × 18 km
**Last VEI 8**: ~760,000 years ago (Bishop Tuff eruption)

**760,000 year eruption**:
- 600 km³ ejected
- Ash reached Nebraska
- Created current caldera

**Current status**:
- Seismic swarms (ongoing since 1980)
- Ground uplift in resurgent dome
- CO₂ emissions kill trees in some areas
- Mammoth Mountain ski resort on flanks
- Closely monitored by USGS

#### 6. Valles Caldera (New Mexico, USA)

**Location**: Jemez Mountains, New Mexico
**Caldera size**: 22 km diameter
**Last VEI 8**: ~1.25 million years ago

**Current status**:
- Dormant
- Geothermal features (hot springs)
- No signs of reactivation
- National preserve

#### 7. Aira Caldera (Japan)

**Location**: Kyushu, Japan (Kagoshima Bay)
**Caldera size**: 20 km diameter
**Last VEI 7**: ~30,000 years ago

**Current status**:
- Sakurajima volcano erupts from caldera rim (hundreds of times/year)
- 600,000+ people live around caldera
- Active monitoring

### Additional Potential Supervolcanoes

- **Atitlán Caldera** (Guatemala): VEI 7 eruption 84,000 years ago
- **Lake Taupo** (New Zealand): Multiple VEI 7-8 events
- **Whakamaru** (New Zealand): VEI 8 eruption 340,000 years ago
- **Kurile Lake** (Russia): VEI 7 eruption 7,700 years ago

### Could a Supervolcano Erupt Soon?

**Short answer**: Extremely unlikely in our lifetimes.

**Why**:
- VEI 8 eruptions occur roughly every **50,000-100,000 years globally**
- Most known supervolcanoes show no signs of imminent eruption
- Modern monitoring can detect magma movement years to decades before eruption
- Even Yellowstone, often sensationalized, shows no signs of impending catastrophic eruption

**Warning signs we'd see**:
- Massive earthquake swarms (thousands per day)
- Rapid ground deformation (meters, not centimeters)
- Changes in geothermal features
- Gas emissions increasing dramatically
- Magma detected moving upward

**The real risk**: Smaller VEI 5-6 eruptions from active volcanoes are **far more likely** and still devastating.

### Supervolcano vs. "Regular" Volcanic Disasters

**Supervolcanoes are rare**. The deadliest volcanic disasters involve smaller eruptions near populations:
- Nevado del Ruiz (1985): VEI 3, killed 23,000
- Mount Pelée (1902): VEI 4, killed 29,000
- Krakatoa (1883): VEI 6, killed 36,000

**Bottom line**: While supervolcanoes are fascinating and potentially catastrophic, active "normal" volcanoes pose the immediate threat to human populations.

## The Volcanic Future

Volcanic activity will continue as long as Earth's interior remains hot and tectonic plates keep moving. Current monitoring suggests:

- No signs of imminent supervolcano eruptions
- Ring of Fire will remain the most active region
- Climate change may influence volcanic activity (ice melt reduces pressure on magma systems)
- Improved monitoring reduces risk to populations
